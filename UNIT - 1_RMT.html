Fundamental Equations for Free Space Propagation

Free space propagation refers to the transmission of electromagnetic waves through an unobstructed vacuum or air. The propagation of radio waves in free space is governed by several fundamental equations.

  1. Friis Transmission Equation: This equation calculates the power received by an antenna from another antenna that is transmitting in free space.

    [Pr=Pt×(λ4πd)2×Gt×Gr][ P_r = P_t \times \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2 \times G_t \times G_r ][Pr​=Pt​×(4πdλ​)2×Gt​×Gr​]﻿

    where:

    • (Pr)( P_r )(Pr​)﻿ = Received power
    • (Pt)( P_t )(Pt​)﻿ = Transmitted power
    • (λ)( \lambda ) (λ)﻿= Wavelength of the signal
    • (d)( d )(d)﻿ = Distance between the antennas
    • (Gt)( G_t )(Gt​)﻿ = Gain of the transmitting antenna
    • (Gr) ( G_r ) (Gr​)﻿= Gain of the receiving antenna

    The Friis transmission equation describes the power received by an antenna under idealized conditions given another antenna some distance away transmitting a known amount of power. It's a key equation in understanding free-space propagation in radio communications.

    Friis Transmission Equation Derivation

    To derive the Friis transmission equation, let's start with a few assumptions and concepts:

    1. Isotropic Radiator: We assume that the transmitting antenna is an isotropic radiator, meaning it radiates power uniformly in all directions.
    1. Free Space: There is no obstruction between the transmitting and receiving antennas, so the propagation is purely in free space.
    1. Far-Field Region: The receiver is in the far-field region of the transmitter, which means the distance between them is large compared to the wavelength of the signal.

    Derivation Steps

    1. Power Density at a Distance:

      Assume a transmitting antenna radiates power \(P_t\) uniformly in all directions. At a distance \(d\) from the antenna, the power is spread over the surface of a sphere with radius \(d\). The surface area \(A\) of a sphere is given by:


      A = 4 \pi d^2

      The power density \(S\) (power per unit area) at distance \(d\) is then:



      S=PtA=Pt4πd2S = \frac{P_t}{A} = \frac{P_t}{4 \pi d^2}S=APt​​=4πd2Pt​​﻿

    1. Power Received by the Antenna:

      A receiving antenna placed at distance \(d\) from the transmitting antenna will intercept a portion of the power. The amount of power it intercepts depends on its effective area \(A_r\). The effective area \(A_r\) is related to the antenna gain \(G_r\) and the wavelength \(\lambda\) of the signal by:


      Ar=Grλ24πA_r = \frac{G_r \lambda^2}{4 \pi}Ar​=4πGr​λ2​﻿

      The power received \(P_r\) by the receiving antenna is the power density \(S\) at distance \(d\) times the effective area \(A_r\):



      Pr=S∗Ar=(Pt4πd2)(Grλ24π)P_r = S * A_r = \left(\frac{P_t}{4 \pi d^2}\right) \left(\frac{G_r \lambda^2}{4 \pi}\right)Pr​=S∗Ar​=(4πd2Pt​​)(4πGr​λ2​)﻿

    1. Incorporating Transmitter Gain:

      If the transmitting antenna has a gain \(G_t\), it focuses the power in a particular direction, increasing the power density in that direction by \(G_t\) times. Thus, the power density \(S'\) with a gain \(G_t\) at distance \(d\) becomes:



      S′=PtGt4πd2S' = \frac{P_t G_t}{4 \pi d^2}S′=4πd2Pt​Gt​​﻿

      Substituting \(S'\) into the received power equation:



      Pr=(PtGt4πd2)(Grλ24π)P_r = \left(\frac{P_t G_t}{4 \pi d^2}\right) \left(\frac{G_r \lambda^2}{4 \pi}\right)Pr​=(4πd2Pt​Gt​​)(4πGr​λ2​)﻿

    1. Simplifying the Equation:

      Combine all terms:



      Pr=PtGtGrλ2(4πd)2P_r = \frac{P_t G_t G_r \lambda^2}{(4 \pi d)^2}Pr​=(4πd)2Pt​Gt​Gr​λ2​﻿

      This is the Friis transmission equation. It shows that the received power \(P_r\) depends on the transmitted power \(P_t\), the gains of the transmitting and receiving antennas \(G_t\) and \(G_r\), the wavelength of the signal \(\lambda\), and the distance between the antennas \(d\).

    Final Form of the Friis Transmission Equation


    Pr=PtGtGrλ2(4πd)2P_r = \frac{P_t G_t G_r \lambda^2}{(4 \pi d)^2}Pr​=(4πd)2Pt​Gt​Gr​λ2​﻿


    or alternatively, in terms of frequency \(f\) (since \(\lambda = \frac{c}{f}\), where \(c\) is the speed of light):



    Pr=PtGtGr(c/f)2(4πd)2=PtGtGrc2(4πfd)2P_r = \frac{P_t G_t G_r (c/f)^2}{(4 \pi d)^2} = \frac{P_t G_t G_r c^2}{(4 \pi f d)^2}Pr​=(4πd)2Pt​Gt​Gr​(c/f)2​=(4πfd)2Pt​Gt​Gr​c2​﻿

    This equation is valid for free-space propagation in the far-field region and helps engineers calculate the expected received signal strength in wireless communication systems.

Definition of Antenna

An antenna is a device that converts electrical power into electromagnetic waves and vice versa. It is a critical component in wireless communication systems, allowing the transmission and reception of radio signals.

Radiation Mechanism of Antennas

Voltage Source → Potential Difference → Electric Field → Free electrons charged → current → magnetic field → electromagnetic wave propagation.

The radiation mechanism of an antenna refers to how it emits or receives electromagnetic waves. This process can be summarized as:

  • Current Flow: Alternating current flows through the antenna, creating a time-varying magnetic field.
  • Electromagnetic Waves: The time-varying magnetic field induces an electric field, and together they propagate as electromagnetic waves.
  • Far-Field Radiation: At distances much greater than the wavelength, the electromagnetic waves form a plane wave, which is the far-field region.

Types of Antennas

Antennas can be classified based on their shape, size, and operational principles. Some common types include:

  1. Dipole Antenna: A simple antenna consisting of two conductive elements. It is one of the most widely used antennas for RF communication.
  1. Monopole Antenna: A half-dipole antenna with one end connected to the ground plane. It is commonly used for mobile communications.
  1. Yagi-Uda Antenna: Consists of a driven element and several parasitic elements. It is directional and used for television reception and amateur radio.
  1. Parabolic Reflector Antenna: Uses a parabolic dish to focus the radio waves into a beam. It is used for satellite and microwave communications.
  1. Patch Antenna: A flat, rectangular antenna that can be mounted on surfaces. It is commonly used in mobile devices and GPS systems.
  1. Horn Antenna: A flared-out metal waveguide that directs radio waves. It is used in radar and microwave applications.
  1. Log-Periodic Antenna: A broadband antenna with elements of different lengths, used in wideband communication.

Performance Parameters of Antennas

  1. Radiation Pattern: The graphical representation of the radiation properties of an antenna as a function of space coordinates. It shows the relative strength of the radiated field in different directions.
  1. Directivity: The measure of how concentrated the radiation is in a particular direction. It is the ratio of the radiation intensity in the direction of maximum radiation to the average radiation intensity.

    D=UmaxUavgD = \frac{U_{\text{max}}}{U_{\text{avg}}}D=Uavg​Umax​​﻿

    where:

    • Umax U_{\text{max}} Umax​﻿ = Maximum radiation intensity
    • \UavgU_{\text{avg}}Uavg​﻿ = Average radiation intensity
  1. Gain: The measure of the ability of the antenna to direct or concentrate radio frequency energy in a particular direction. Gain takes into account the efficiency and directivity of the antenna.

    G=η×D G = \eta \times DG=η×D﻿

    where:

    • \( G \) = Gain
    • η\etaη﻿ = Efficiency of the antenna
    • \( D \) = Directivity
  1. Efficiency: The ratio of the power radiated by the antenna to the total power input to the antenna. Efficiency considers losses due to heat, impedance mismatch, etc.

    η=PradPinput\eta = \frac{P_{\text{rad}}}{P_{\text{input}}}η=Pinput​Prad​​﻿

    where:

    • \( P_{\text{rad}} \) = Radiated power
    • \( P_{\text{input}} \) = Input power
  1. Half Power Beam Width (HPBW): The angular width of the main lobe of the radiation pattern at half the maximum power point. It indicates the beamwidth or directivity of the antenna.
  1. Bandwidth: The range of frequencies over which the antenna can operate effectively. It is defined as the difference between the highest and lowest frequencies for which the antenna maintains acceptable performance.
  1. Polarization: The orientation of the electric field vector of the electromagnetic wave radiated by the antenna. Common types include linear, circular, and elliptical polarization.
  1. Input Impedance: The impedance presented by the antenna at its terminals. It is a complex quantity and consists of resistance (real part) and reactance (imaginary part).
  1. Radiation Efficiency: A measure of how effectively the antenna converts input power into radiated power.

    Radiation Efficiency=Radiated PowerInput Power \text{Radiation Efficiency} = \frac{\text{Radiated Power}}{\text{Input Power}} Radiation Efficiency=Input PowerRadiated Power​﻿

  1. Effective Length: A measure of the length of the antenna that contributes to its radiation. It is a theoretical concept used in antenna analysis.
  1. Effective Area (Aperture): The area of an antenna that intercepts the power of an incoming electromagnetic wave. It is related to the gain and wavelength of the antenna.


    Ae=Gλ24πA_e = \frac{G \lambda^2}{4\pi}Ae​=4πGλ2​﻿

    where:

    • \( A_e \) = Effective area
    • \( G \) = Gain
    • λ\lambdaλ﻿ = Wavelength
  1. Radiation Sphere: A conceptual sphere around an antenna from which the radiation is considered. It is used to analyze and calculate radiation properties such as intensity and power.

Understanding these fundamental concepts is crucial for designing efficient and effective antenna systems for various communication and signal processing applications.
